Learning Objectives

What is the Accounting Cycle?

The Accounting Cycle is the systematic process of recording, processing, and summarizing financial transactions of a business. It begins when a transaction occurs and ends when financial statements are prepared and the books are closed. This cycle repeats every accounting period (monthly, quarterly, or annually).

The purpose of the accounting cycle is to ensure accuracy, completeness, and organization in financial reporting.

Importance of the Accounting Cycle

  • Ensures accurate financial records by following a standardized process.
  • Helps detect errors and fraud through regular reviewing of entries.
  • Organizes transactions to make financial statements easier to prepare.
  • Supports decision-making by providing updated and reliable financial information.
  • Ensures compliance with BIR, SEC, and other regulatory requirements.
  • Improves financial control over assets, liabilities, and expenses.

Steps in the Accounting Cycle

1. Identifying Transactions

The first step is to identify all business transactions that can be measured in monetary terms. These include sales, purchases, expenses, collections, and investments.

2. Recording Transactions in the General Journal

Using the double-entry bookkeeping system, each transaction is recorded as a debit and a credit. Journal entries contain the date, accounts affected, amounts, and explanation.

Example Journal Entry: Debit: Office Supplies ‚Ç±2,000 Credit: Cash ‚Ç±2,000

3. Posting to the General Ledger

All journal entries are transferred (posted) to the ledger‚Äîwhere each account has its own page or section. This step organizes financial data by account.

Example: All entries affecting ‚ÄúCash‚Äù are grouped under the Cash ledger.

4. Preparing the Trial Balance

After posting, a Trial Balance is prepared to check if total debits equal total credits. If they do not match, errors must be corrected before proceeding.

5. Making Adjusting Entries

At the end of the period, adjusting entries are made to update accounts for expenses incurred but not yet recorded, revenues earned but not recorded, and depreciation.

  • Accrued Expenses ‚Äì expenses earned but unpaid
  • Accrued Income ‚Äì income earned but uncollected
  • Prepaid Expenses ‚Äì adjusting for used portions
  • Unearned Income ‚Äì converting to earned revenue
  • Depreciation ‚Äì allocating asset cost over time

6. Preparing the Adjusted Trial Balance

After adjustments, a new trial balance is prepared. This ensures the books are still balanced and ready for financial statement preparation.

7. Preparing Financial Statements

From the Adjusted Trial Balance, the four major financial statements are prepared:

  • Income Statement ‚Äì shows profit or loss.
  • Statement of Owner‚Äôs Equity ‚Äì shows changes in capital.
  • Balance Sheet ‚Äì presents assets, liabilities, and equity.
  • Cash Flow Statement ‚Äì shows cash inflows and outflows.

8. Closing Entries

Temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, withdrawals) are closed at the end of the period and transferred to the Owner‚Äôs Capital account. This resets the temporary accounts to zero for the next cycle.

9. Preparing the Post-Closing Trial Balance

Only permanent accounts remain (assets, liabilities, capital). This ensures the ledger is ready for the next accounting period.

Summary

The accounting cycle ensures that financial transactions are recorded, organized, adjusted, reported, and closed accurately. It helps businesses maintain reliable financial information, detect errors, and prepare essential financial statements. Mastering the accounting cycle is a crucial skill for all accounting students and future professionals.
