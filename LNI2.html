Lesson 2 ‚Äì Common Forms of Negotiable Instruments

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  • Identify the common forms of negotiable instruments such as promissory notes, bills of exchange, and cheques.
  • Explain the roles of the parties involved in each type of instrument.
  • Describe the purpose and use of each form of negotiable instrument.
  • Differentiate a cheque from other bills of exchange.
  • Understand the significance of these instruments in business transactions.

1. Promissory Notes

A promissory note is a signed written promise by one party (the debtor or maker) to pay a specified sum of money to another party (the creditor or payee) on a particular date or on demand.

Specimen Characteristics

It contains an unconditional promise to pay a fixed amount.

Parties to a Promissory Note

  • Maker (Drawer/Debtor) ‚Äì the person who promises to pay.
  • Payee (Creditor) ‚Äì the person to whom payment is to be made.

2. Bill of Exchange

A bill of exchange is a written unconditional order by one party (drawer) directing another (drawee) to pay a certain amount either on demand or at a future date.

It contains an order from the creditor to the debtor to pay a third person.

Parties to a Bill of Exchange

  • Drawer ‚Äì the person who orders payment.
  • drawee ‚Äì the person ordered to pay.
  • Payee ‚Äì the person who receives payment.

3. Cheque

A cheque is a special type of bill of exchange drawn on a banker and payable only on demand.

Important characteristics:

  • It is always drawn on a specified banker.
  • It is always payable on demand.
  • It must be signed by the drawer.

Parties to a Cheque

  • Drawer ‚Äì the person who writes the cheque.
  • Drawee ‚Äì the bank on which the cheque is drawn.
  • Payee ‚Äì the person entitled to receive payment.
