Introduction

Every negotiable instrument involves distinct parties who assume rights and obligations under the Negotiable Instruments Law. Knowing who these parties are and the nature of their liabilities helps determine who is responsible if the instrument is dishonored.

Parties to a Promissory Note

A promissory note is a written and signed promise by one person to pay another a definite sum of money on demand or at a future time.

Maker (Drawer)

The maker is the person who promises to pay and bears primary liability.

Payee

The payee is the person to whom payment is to be made and is the initial holder.

Endorser / Endorsee

If negotiated, endorsers transfer the instrument and endorsees become new holders. Endorsers assume secondary liability unless otherwise qualified.

Example: Mr. Santos makes a note promising to pay Ms. Reyes ‚Ç±50,000 on a specific date. Mr. Santos (maker) is primarily liable to the holder of the note.

Parties to a Bill of Exchange

A bill of exchange is an order by one person directing another to pay a specified sum to a third person, either on demand or at a future date.

Drawer

The drawer issues the bill and is secondarily liable (liable if drawee/acceptor fails to pay).

Drawee / Acceptor

When the drawee accepts by signing, they become the acceptor and assume primary liability to pay the bill.

Payee

The payee is the person entitled to receive payment.

Other Parties

Endorsers, endorsees and holders in due course may be involved through negotiation.

Example: If Mr. Lim (drawee) signs ‚ÄúAccepted,‚Äù he becomes primarily liable. If he fails to pay, the drawer and endorsers may be pursued after notice of dishonor is given.

Parties to a Cheque

A cheque is a specialized bill of exchange drawn on a bank and payable on demand.

Drawer

The account holder who writes the cheque. Drawer is generally secondarily liable if the cheque is dishonored (e.g., insufficient funds).

Drawee (Bank)

The bank on which the cheque is drawn; the bank must pay when the cheque is properly presented and funds are available.

Payee

The person to whom payment is directed; if negotiated, endorsers and endorsees may be introduced.

Example: If ABC Bank refuses payment due to insufficient funds, the payee can demand payment from the drawer who is secondarily liable.

Classification of Liabilities

  • Primary Liability: Obligation of the party absolutely bound to pay (e.g., maker of promissory note, acceptor of bill).
  • Secondary Liability: Obligation of parties to pay only if instrument is dishonored (e.g., drawer, endorsers).

Dishonor of Negotiable Instruments

A negotiable instrument is dishonored when the party primarily liable refuses to accept (non-acceptance) or pay (non-payment) the instrument.

  • Dishonor by Non-Acceptance: Drawee refuses to accept a bill when presented for acceptance.
  • Dishonor by Non-Payment: Maker, acceptor, or drawee fails to pay on maturity.

Upon dishonor, the holder should issue a notice of dishonor to parties secondarily liable to preserve rights of recourse. Failure to give timely notice may discharge secondary parties.

Discharge of Parties

Parties may be discharged from liability by:

  • Payment in due course by or on behalf of the primarily liable party.
  • Cancellation or intentional destruction of the instrument by the holder.
  • Release or waiver of rights by agreement.
  • Merger ‚Äî when the principal debtor becomes the holder in his own right.

Importance in Commercial Transactions

Clear understanding of the roles and liabilities of parties to negotiable instruments promotes certainty and confidence in commercial dealings. It ensures that creditors know where to seek payment and that parties understand the implications of endorsement, presentment, and dishonor.
