What are Accounting Principles?

Accounting principles are the rules, concepts, and guidelines that govern how financial transactions are recorded, classified, and reported. These principles ensure that financial statements are accurate, consistent, and comparable across different periods and businesses.

Common Accounting Principles include:

  • Revenue Recognition Principle ‚Äì Record revenue when earned, not when cash is received.
  • Matching Principle ‚Äì Match expenses with the revenues they help generate.
  • Cost Principle ‚Äì Record assets at their original cost.
  • Conservatism Principle ‚Äì Record expenses and liabilities as soon as possible, but record revenues only when assured.

Purpose of Accounting Principles

  • Ensure Consistency ‚Äì Financial records follow standard rules for easy comparison across periods.
  • Improve Accuracy and Reliability ‚Äì Ensures all financial data is recorded correctly.
  • Provide Useful Information ‚Äì Helps owners, managers, investors, and creditors make informed decisions.
  • Prevent Manipulation ‚Äì Builds trust by avoiding misleading financial reporting.
  • Support Compliance ‚Äì Helps businesses follow GAAP or IFRS regulations.

What is GAAP?

GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) is a set of standard accounting rules and guidelines used in preparing financial statements. GAAP ensures consistency, reliability, and comparability across businesses.

Importance of GAAP:

  • Consistency ‚Äì Same reporting methods across companies.
  • Reliability ‚Äì Financial information is truthful and accurate.
  • Comparability ‚Äì Useful for comparing performance across businesses.
  • Transparency ‚Äì Prevents fraud and misrepresentation.
  • Regulatory Compliance ‚Äì Required by BIR, SEC, and other agencies.
  • Better Decision-Making ‚Äì Helps investors and managers evaluate the company.

Fundamental Accounting Concepts

  • Business Entity Concept ‚Äì The business is separate from the owner.
  • Going Concern Concept ‚Äì The business will continue operating indefinitely.
  • Monetary Unit Concept ‚Äì All transactions are recorded in one currency.
  • Time Period Concept ‚Äì Reports are prepared for specific time intervals.
  • Cost Principle ‚Äì Assets are recorded based on original cost.
  • Matching Principle ‚Äì Expenses must match the revenues they generate.
  • Full Disclosure Principle ‚Äì All relevant information must be reported.
  • Objectivity Principle ‚Äì Records must be based on verifiable evidence.

Application of Concepts in Real Business Situations

  • Business Entity Concept ‚Äì Personal expenses are not included in business records.
  • Going Concern Concept ‚Äì Long-term assets are recorded at cost and depreciated over time.
