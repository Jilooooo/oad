Introduction

Agile methodologies provide flexible, adaptive frameworks for managing projects. Two of the most widely used Agile methods are Scrum and Kanban. Both improve team collaboration, transparency, and efficiency, but they work differently depending on the needs of the project.

What is Scrum Methodology?

Scrum is an Agile framework used to manage complex projects by dividing tasks into short, time-boxed cycles called Sprints (usually 2‚Äì4 weeks). Scrum focuses on collaboration, continuous improvement, and delivering small, functional parts of the project regularly.

Key Roles in Scrum

  • Product Owner: Represents customers, manages the product backlog, and prioritizes work.
  • Scrum Master: Ensures the team follows Scrum principles and removes obstacles.
  • Development Team: The group responsible for creating the actual product during the Sprint.

Scrum Events (Ceremonies)

  • Sprint Planning: Team sets goals and selects tasks for the next Sprint.
  • Daily Scrum (Stand-up): A short 15-minute meeting to check progress and identify issues.
  • Sprint Review: Completed work is demonstrated to stakeholders.
  • Sprint Retrospective: Team reflects on the Sprint and identifies improvements.

Scrum Board Example

A typical Scrum board includes:

  • Product Backlog
  • Sprint Backlog
  • In Progress
  • Testing
  • Done

What is Kanban Methodology?

Kanban is a visual workflow management system that uses a board with columns (e.g., To Do ‚Üí In Progress ‚Üí Done) to help teams track tasks, manage workflow, and prevent bottlenecks. The term "Kanban" means "signboard" in Japanese, emphasizing visibility and transparency.

Key Features of Kanban

  • Visual Workflow: Tasks are displayed on a Kanban board.
  • Work-in-Progress (WIP) Limits: Controls how many tasks can be in progress at once.
  • Continuous Flow: Work moves smoothly, without sprints or fixed iterations.
  • Improvement Over Time: The team adjusts processes based on observed patterns.

Scrum vs Kanban

Scrum Kanban
Uses Sprints (fixed time periods) Continuous flow (no fixed time)
Strict roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner) No specific required roles
Work planned per Sprint Work added anytime if capacity allows
Daily Scrum meetings Meetings only when needed
Better for complex product development Better for maintenance or continuous tasks

Summary

Scrum and Kanban are powerful Agile methodologies used to improve workflow, teamwork, and project efficiency. Scrum divides work into structured Sprints with defined roles and ceremonies, while Kanban focuses on visual management and continuous flow. Both methods help teams adapt to change, deliver high-quality output, and enhance overall productivity.
