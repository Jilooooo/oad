Lesson 3 – Filing, Docketing, and Record Management

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of filing, docketing, and record management in a legal office.
  • Identify different types of filing systems used in law offices.
  • Describe the steps involved in docketing and why the process is important.
  • Understand proper storage, security, and retrieval procedures for legal documents.
  • Recognize the importance of maintaining organized and complete client files.
  • Apply best practices in organizing case folders and managing office records.

Introduction

Filing, docketing, and record management are essential tasks in a legal office. Hundreds of documents, pleadings, and case files are handled daily, making it important to have an organized system for storing, tracking, and retrieving legal records. Proper record management prevents errors, protects confidentiality, and supports efficient case handling.

Filing System in Legal Offices

A well-organized filing system ensures documents are stored safely and retrieved quickly. It prevents delays, avoids misplacement, and helps lawyers access case information easily.

Types of Filing Systems

1. Alphabetical Filing System

Files are arranged alphabetically by client or case name.

  • Cruz, Juan
  • Dela Cruz, Maria
  • Santos, Pedro

2. Numerical Filing System

  • Each file is assigned a number (e.g., 001, 002, 003).
  • Files are arranged by number instead of name.

3. Chronological Filing System

  • Files are arranged by date—month, day, or year.
  • Useful for organizing documents based on when they were filed or created.

4. Case-Based Filing System

  • Documents are grouped by specific case.
  • Each folder contains all pleadings, evidence, and correspondence for one case.

Purpose of Filing Systems

  • Fast and accurate retrieval of documents.
  • Organized and complete case records.
  • Prevents loss or duplication of files.
  • Protects confidential client information.

Docketing Procedures

Docketing is the official process of assigning a case number and recording case details when a new case is filed. It serves as the office’s permanent record of each case.

Steps in Docketing

  • 1. Receive Case Information: Collect the names of parties, case type, and filing date.
  • 2. Assign a Case Number: Example: Civil Case No. 2025-001.
  • 3. Record in Docket Book/System: Enter:
    • Case number
    • Case title (Plaintiff vs. Defendant)
    • Date filed
    • Nature of the case
    • Assigned lawyer
    • Status and remarks
  • 4. Update Regularly: Record hearings, motions, and decisions.

Purpose of Docketing

  • Track case progress efficiently.
  • Avoid confusion between similar cases.
  • Monitor deadlines and schedules.

Storage, Security, and Retrieval of Legal Documents

1. Storage

  • Use labeled cabinets and folders for physical files.
  • Use computers or cloud storage for digital files.
  • Group case folders by category (civil, criminal, corporate, etc.).

2. Security

  • Allow access only to authorized personnel.
  • Lock physical cabinets and password-protect files.
  • Backup important files regularly.
  • Maintain strict confidentiality.

3. Retrieval

  • Use a reference log or index card system.
  • Record when files are borrowed or returned.
  • Use color coding or labels for easier identification.

Maintaining Client Files and Case Folders

Each client or case must have its own organized folder containing all relevant documents from the beginning to the end of the case.

Contents of a Client File

  • Client Information Sheet
  • Correspondence (emails, letters, memos)
  • Pleadings and court documents
  • Evidence and attachments
  • Billing statements and receipts
  • Case progress notes

Tips for Maintaining Case Folders

  • Label folders clearly (e.g., Juan Dela Cruz vs. Pedro Santos – Civil Case No. 2025-003).
  • Arrange documents chronologically or by category.
  • Keep files neat, updated, and secure.
  • Move closed cases to the archive for long-term storage.

What is the purpose of filing systems?

  • To make documents harder to find
  • To organize and retrieve documents quickly
  • To replace digital storage
  • To increase paperwork

What does docketing do?

  • Destroys old case files
  • Assigns case numbers and records case details
  • Creates new courtrooms
  • Files client receipts

Which filing system arranges files by date?

  • Numerical
  • Alphabetical
  • Chronological
  • Random

What should be restricted to authorized personnel only?

  • Calendars
  • Legal documents and case files
  • Office supplies
  • Public newsletters

Which is included in a client file?

  • Restaurant menus
  • Random photos
  • Pleadings, evidence, correspondence
  • Personal diaries

Summary

Filing, docketing, and record management ensure the legal office operates smoothly and efficiently. Proper systems protect documents, maintain confidentiality, and help lawyers track case progress. Mastering these procedures is essential for accurate, organized, and professional legal office work.
