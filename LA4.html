What are Financial Statements?

Financial statements are formal records that summarize the financial activities and position of a business over a specific period. They are prepared based on accounting records and show how the business performed financially and its current financial standing.

Main Financial Statements

  • Income Statement ‚Äì shows revenues, expenses, and net income or loss.
  • Statement of Owner‚Äôs Equity ‚Äì shows changes in capital, investments, withdrawals, and net income (loss).
  • Balance Sheet ‚Äì shows assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific date.
  • Cash Flow Statement ‚Äì shows cash inflows and outflows (optional for small businesses).

Purpose of Financial Statements

  • Provide information for decision-making ‚Äì helps owners, managers, creditors, and investors make informed choices.
  • Monitor financial performance ‚Äì shows profitability and financial health.
  • Ensure accountability and transparency ‚Äì supports trust among stakeholders.
  • Assist in planning and budgeting ‚Äì provides data for future strategies.
  • Compliance ‚Äì meets legal and regulatory requirements (BIR, SEC, etc.).

Types of Financial Statements

  • Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet) ‚Äì shows assets, liabilities, and equity.
  • Income Statement ‚Äì shows revenues and expenses to determine net income or loss.
  • Statement of Owner‚Äôs Equity ‚Äì shows changes in owner‚Äôs capital.
  • Cash Flow Statement ‚Äì shows cash received and cash spent.

Characteristics of Good Financial Reports

1. Relevance

  • Information must influence decision-making.
  • Helps users evaluate past, present, or future events.
  • Example: Revenue trends help determine business expansion.

2. Reliability

  • Information must be accurate, complete, and free of bias.
  • Example: Cash records that match bank statements.

3. Comparability

  • Allows comparison across different periods or companies.
  • Example: Using the same inventory valuation method each year.

4. Understandability

  • Information should be clear and easy to interpret.
  • Presented logically for users with basic accounting knowledge.
  • Example: Classifying assets as current or non-current.
